Latency ⇄ throughput

They're independent axes — but push on one and the other pushes back. The trade-off, the law that ties them together with concurrency, and the percentiles that expose what averages hide.

Ask two engineers "is this service fast?" and you'll get two different answers — because one is thinking about latency (how long a single request takes) and the other is thinking about throughput (how many requests finish per second). Both are right. Both are talking past each other. This page is about what happens when you try to move both numbers at once: the trade-off, the law that forces them to agree, and the distribution they hide behind.

01Two sides of "fast"

Imagine a highway. There are two very different questions you can ask about it:

How long is my drive?

From the on-ramp to your exit, how many minutes does it take? This is latency — the time a single trip takes from start to finish.

How many cars pass per minute?

Standing on an overpass, how many cars cross below you every minute? This is throughput — the rate at which the system completes work.

These are independent. A highway can have a short drive but few cars (fast and empty). It can have many cars and a short drive (fast and busy — the ideal). It can have a long drive and few cars (broken). Or many cars and a long drive (congested). Four quadrants, all real.

The trouble is that most performance work doesn't let you live in any one quadrant in peace. Push on throughput and latency starts creeping up. Push on latency and throughput starts shrinking. The rest of this page is about why that happens, and about the one equation that forces the two numbers — plus a third, concurrency — to always agree.

A trap worth naming
"Low latency" and "high throughput" are not the same goal. A system can have very low latency and very low throughput (a single-threaded server answering one slow client instantly). It can have enormous throughput and terrible latency (a batch job that processes a billion rows in an hour, but any individual row sits in the queue for 40 minutes). Different workloads care about different numbers — and optimizing for the wrong one is how systems get designed for the wrong use case.

02Why optimizing one often hurts the other

There's a natural tension. Techniques that increase throughput tend to add latency, and techniques that reduce latency tend to cap throughput.

Batching: the classic example

Say your service writes events to a database. You can either:

Write each event immediately

Every event pays the full round-trip cost to the DB. Latency per event is ~5 ms. But each call is overhead-heavy, so you max out at maybe 2,000 events/sec.

Buffer and flush in batches of 100

Events now wait up to, say, 50 ms in the buffer before being flushed. Latency per event is 50+ ms. But a single batched write pushes 100 rows, so throughput jumps to 20,000+ events/sec.

Batching trades latency for throughput. You've accepted that every event waits a bit, in exchange for moving many more events overall. This is why analytics pipelines batch aggressively, and why payment APIs do not.

Concurrency: the other lever

Handling more requests in parallel raises throughput — until the shared resources (CPU cores, DB connections, locks) saturate. At that point, new requests start queuing, and queuing time is added to every request's latency. Throughput flattens; latency climbs. The system is now overloaded, and pushing more work in only makes it worse.

Figure 01 — The latency–throughput curve

As offered load grows, latency stays flat — until the system nears its capacity. Past that "knee," every extra request has to wait behind others. Throughput stops rising and latency goes vertical. Good capacity planning keeps you safely left of the knee.

The knee isn't a specific number you can calculate ahead of time — it depends on the workload, the hardware, and the shared resources in the system. But every system has one, and operating near it is risky: a small traffic spike tips you over into the overloaded zone where latency increases are no longer recoverable without shedding load.

03Little's Law — the ironclad link

There's a simple equation that connects latency, throughput, and how much work is in-flight at once. It holds for any stable system — queue, server, entire pipeline — without any assumptions about distribution or scheduling. It's called Little's Law, and it's the most useful formula in performance engineering.

L = λ × W

In words: the average number of items in the system (L) equals the arrival rate (λ) times the average time each item spends inside (W). Read another way: concurrency = throughput × latency.

Figure 02 — Little's Law, visually

If 200 requests arrive per second and each spends 40 ms inside the system, there are on average 8 requests in flight at any moment. The three numbers cannot move independently — fix any two and the third is forced.

Why it's so useful

Little's Law lets you sanity-check capacity claims without benchmarking. Some examples:

  • "We handle 10,000 RPS with 50 ms average latency." → average in-flight = 500. If your thread pool has 200 threads, that claim is impossible.
  • "We need to serve 1,000 RPS and each request takes 200 ms." → you need at least 200 concurrent workers. Not 10. Not 50. Two hundred.
  • "Latency shot up 4× but RPS is flat." → L must have grown 4×. Work is piling up somewhere — find the queue that's filling.

Any time two of the three numbers seem suspicious together, the third exposes the lie. This is also why the trade-off in the previous section is real and not a coincidence: if you want higher throughput (λ ↑) without adding concurrency (L fixed — thread pool size, connection pool size, whatever), latency (W) must drop. There's no fourth option. The equation forces it.

04Why averages lie

So far every latency number we've discussed has been an average. Averages are seductive because they reduce thousands of requests to one tidy number. They're also dangerous because real latency distributions are rarely symmetric. They have a tail — a small number of requests that are dramatically slower than typical.

Consider 1,000 requests. 990 complete in 20 ms. 10 complete in 2 seconds (because of a cache miss, a GC pause, a slow network path, a retry). The average latency is:

(990 × 20 ms + 10 × 2000 ms) / 1000 = 39.8 ms

That 39.8 ms looks fine on a dashboard. But 1% of your users waited two full seconds. The average hid them. And if your service is called ten times to render a single page, the chance that at least one of those calls hits the slow path is much higher — that's the infamous tail-at-scale effect.

Averages describe the typical request. Percentiles describe what your users actually experience.

05p95, p99, and the tail

A percentile answers: "What's the worst latency the fastest X% of requests saw?" If p95 = 120 ms, then 95% of requests finished in 120 ms or less. The other 5% were slower. p99, p99.9, p99.99 push further into the tail.

Which percentile matters?

p50 (the median)

The "typical" user experience. Good for sanity, bad for alerts — a regression here is already a disaster.

p95

A common dashboard target. Catches the upper body of the distribution. 1 in 20 requests is worse than this.

p99

Where most latency SLOs live. 1 in 100 requests is worse. This is what power users and synthetic monitors see.

p99.9 and beyond

Critical at scale. If you serve a billion requests a day, p99.9 is still a million slow requests. Fan-out pages hit this tail routinely.

Why p99 matters more than you think
If rendering a single page requires 10 backend calls, and each call has p99 = 500 ms, the chance that at least one call hits the tail is roughly 1 − 0.9910 ≈ 10%. So the page's p90 is whatever the backend's p99 is. This is why fan-out systems — microservices, search, ads — obsess over the tail. Every fanned-out call multiplies exposure to it.

A rule of thumb for tail reduction

You can't average your way out of a bad tail. Common tactics instead:

  • Hedged requests. Send a second copy of the request to another replica if the first hasn't answered by the p95; take whichever returns first. Trades a little extra load for dramatic tail improvement.
  • Timeouts and fallbacks. Cut off outlier requests and serve a degraded response rather than letting them pile up and poison the queue for everyone else.
  • Back-pressure. Reject or shed load before queues grow long, rather than accepting work you can't complete on time. A refused request is better than a queued one that stalls your worker pool.
  • Isolate noisy neighbors. GC pauses, background compactions, and cold caches cause tail spikes. Pin critical paths away from them.

06The one-line takeaway

Latency and throughput are independent axes, but Little's Law forces them to agree with concurrency — and averages hide the tail, so watch p95 and p99 instead.

Those four ideas — the trade-off, Little's Law, the tail, and the percentiles that expose it — are the working vocabulary of systems that handle load. Everything else (queueing theory, capacity planning, load shedding, SLOs, autoscaling) is an application of them. Once you're fluent, "is this system fast?" stops being a vague question and becomes a very specific one: fast for whom, measured at which percentile, under what load, with how much in flight?
