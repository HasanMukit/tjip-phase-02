Back-of-the-Envelope estimation

Five minutes, a few memorized constants, and the discipline to round hard — that's all it takes to size a system before writing a line of code.

Before any system design conversation gets to "which database?" or "Kafka or NATS?", there's a much smaller question hiding underneath it: how big is this thing, actually? A few simple multiplications turn a vague idea ("a feed app") into concrete numbers — 30 K writes per second, 9 TB per year, 2 GB of cache. Once those numbers exist, half the design choices make themselves. This page is about how to get them in five minutes with a pen.

Back-of-the-envelope estimation is the bridge between latency and throughput as vocabulary and capacity planning as a craft. It's also the single most cited skill in system-design interviews — not because the exact answer matters, but because the habit of reaching for it does.

01What "back-of-the-envelope" actually means

It's a quick, paper-only sizing of the system. No benchmarks, no spreadsheets, no profiling — just rough multiplication using a handful of memorized constants. The goal isn't precision. The goal is to land within roughly 10× of reality before you've made any commitments.

That sounds loose, but a 10× answer is enormously more useful than no answer. If a back-of-the-envelope says "5 GB/day of storage," you know SQLite on a single VM is fine. If it says "5 TB/day," you know you're shopping for distributed storage. The two regimes have nothing in common — and you knew which one you were in before opening a single doc page.

The point isn't the answer, it's the regime
Back-of-the-envelope estimation rarely tells you which technology to pick. It tells you which class of technologies is even on the table. Single-machine vs. multi-machine. In-memory vs. on-disk. Sync vs. async. Once the regime is fixed, the rest of the design has a much smaller search space.

02The numbers worth memorizing

Three short tables. If you internalize these, almost every BoE calculation collapses into two or three multiplications.

Powers of 2 (storage and counts)

Engineers approximate 210 ≈ 103 so the math stays in base 10. The error is ~2% per multiplication, which is well below the 10× tolerance the whole exercise lives within.

Time (the seconds-in-a-day trick)

Almost every estimate routes through one constant: there are roughly 105 seconds in a day. Memorize that and per-day numbers convert to per-second by lopping off five zeros.

Per-day → per-second

1 day = 86,400 s ≈ 105 s. So 1 M events/day ≈ 10 events/s. 100 M events/day ≈ 1 K events/s. 10 B events/day ≈ 100 K events/s. The conversion is "÷ 105" and you can do it in your head.

Larger windows

1 month ≈ 2.5 × 106 s. 1 year ≈ 3 × 107 s (more precisely 31.5 M). So "events per year" is "events per day × 365" or "per second × 30 M" — pick whichever multiplication is easier.

Typical sizes (what one record actually weighs)

You can't estimate storage without a guess for the size of one row. These ballparks cover most cases:

For latency constants — RAM, SSD, network round-trips — the canonical reference is the 1,000× hierarchy. Memorize those numbers separately; they're the fourth table.

03The four-step method

Almost every BoE problem follows the same shape. Run through these four steps in order and the answer falls out.

Figure 01 — The funnel from users to required capacity

Each step narrows the funnel. Storage and bandwidth come from the same four numbers — multiply by record size for storage, by payload size for bandwidth, by retention years for total disk.

Step 1 — State assumptions out loud

Write down the inputs before you do any math. Daily Active Users (DAU). Actions per user per day. Average payload size. Read-to-write ratio. Retention period. These are guesses — but explicit guesses you can defend, not vibes. The discipline matters because every later number traces back to one of these. If a reviewer disagrees with your conclusion, the disagreement is almost always about an assumption, not the arithmetic.

Step 2 — Compute daily totals

Multiply DAU by actions per user. That's daily writes. For reads, multiply by the read-to-write ratio (typically 10:1 for content systems, 100:1 for social feeds, 1000:1 for read-heavy news sites).

Step 3 — Convert to per-second

Divide by 105. That's it. 100 M actions/day ÷ 105 = 1 K actions/sec. The mental shortcut: lop off five zeros from the daily count.

Step 4 — Apply the peak multiplier

Real systems don't see traffic uniformly across the day. Use a peak factor — typically ×3 for general consumer apps, ×5 for clearly diurnal workloads (commute apps, food delivery), ×2 for global services that average across time zones. The peak number is what you size capacity against; the average is what you bill against.

For storage and bandwidth, two more multiplications close out the calculation:

  • Storage per day = daily writes × bytes per record. Multiply by retention years × 365 for total. Multiply by replication factor (typically 3) for what you actually provision.
  • Bandwidth = peak QPS × payload size. Outbound (to clients) is usually larger than inbound — favor the bigger one.
  • Cache size = the 80/20 rule. 20% of content drives 80% of reads. Size the cache to hold that 20% in RAM.

04Worked example #1 — a Twitter-scale timeline

Designing a service where users post short messages and read a feed of who they follow. Let's size it.

Assumptions

  • DAU = 200 M
  • Each user posts 2 messages per day (avg) and reads 100 messages per day
  • Average message: 280 chars text + ~300 B metadata (author, timestamp, IDs) = ~600 B
  • 30% of messages include a photo at 200 KB
  • Retention: 5 years; replication factor: 3
  • Peak-to-average factor: 3×

Writes per second

200 M users × 2 posts/day = 400 M posts/day. ÷ 105 = 4 K writes/s average. Peak: ~12 K writes/s.

Reads per second

200 M users × 100 reads/day = 20 B reads/day. ÷ 105 = 200 K reads/s average. Peak: ~600 K reads/s. Notice the read load is 50× the write load — this number alone tells you to design read-side caching as the primary lever.

Storage per day

Text: 400 M × 600 B = ~240 GB/day of message data. Photos: 30% × 400 M × 200 KB = ~24 TB/day of media. The photo storage dominates by 100×.

Storage over 5 years (with replication)

Text: 240 GB/day × 365 × 5 × 3 ≈ ~1.3 PB. Photos: 24 TB/day × 365 × 5 × 3 ≈ ~130 PB. The text fits comfortably on a single distributed DB cluster; photos go in object storage (S3-shaped) because no relational store wants 130 PB.

Outbound bandwidth at peak

600 K reads/s × ~600 B per message × ~20 messages/feed-fetch ≈ ~7 GB/s of outbound text. Plus photos when included. This is why CDNs exist — serving 7+ GB/s out of your origin would cost a fortune. Push it to edge caches.

What the numbers told us
Without picking a single technology, the BoE already constrained the design: read-heavy (cache aggressively), media-dominated storage (object store, not RDBMS), CDN-mandatory outbound (origin can't carry it), and shardable hot set (no single-node cache works at this scale). Five minutes of multiplication eliminated half the architectural search space.

05Worked example #2 — YouTube space and cache

Twitter is text-dominant; YouTube is the opposite — video bytes dominate everything else by orders of magnitude, and the interesting BoE questions are how much disk to provision and how to size the cache so most playbacks never touch origin. The math reveals why YouTube is a CDN problem more than a database problem.

Assumptions

  • Upload rate: 500 hours of video per minute (the well-known stat)
  • Average bitrate after encoding (1080p reference): ~50 MB per minute of video
  • YouTube stores 5 quality variants per video → ~5× storage multiplier
  • Replication factor: 3
  • Concurrent peak viewers: ~100 M; average stream bitrate: 2 Mbps
  • Pareto: 20% of videos drive 80% of views

Storage from uploads

500 hours/min × 60 min/hour = 30 K minutes of video uploaded per minute. At 50 MB per minute (single quality): 1.5 TB/min uploaded. Per day: 1.5 TB × 60 × 24 ≈ ~2 PB/day raw.

Multiply by 5 quality variants: ~10 PB/day of stored encoded content. Multiply by replication ×3: ~30 PB/day on disk. Annualized: ~10 EB/year of provisioned storage. There is no single-cluster database that holds 10 EB; YouTube's video bytes live in a dedicated blob store optimized for sequential, write-once-read-many access — not in any general-purpose DB.

Cache sizing — the 80/20 insight

Most videos are watched almost never; a small fraction are watched constantly. The cache only needs to hold the 20% that drives 80% of plays — so the cache size is governed by the popular library, not the total library.

Take the recent window: 30 days × 10 PB/day = ~300 PB of recent multi-quality content. The hot 20% is ~60 PB. That's still vastly too large for a centralized cache, so it's distributed across edge points-of-presence (PoPs):

  • ~1,000 PoPs globally → ~60 TB hot content per PoP. That fits comfortably on flash/NVMe in each edge node.
  • The "very hot" 1% (viral videos, current trending) → ~3 PB total → ~3 TB per PoP in RAM hot tier.

This is the heart of the answer: the hot set fits in edge flash. Origin storage holds ~10 EB; edge caches hold ~60 PB total across 1,000 PoPs; viewer requests almost never round-trip to origin. The numbers prove that the architecture has to be edge-cache-dominated — there's no other way it works at this scale.

Outbound bandwidth (sanity check)

100 M concurrent viewers × 2 Mbps = ~200 Tbps peak outbound. That confirms the same conclusion from a different angle: no origin can serve 200 Tbps; the edge tier carries ~95%+ of bytes and origin handles only cache misses and uploads.

What the numbers told us
Without naming a single product, the BoE forced four conclusions: blob storage, not RDBMS; multi-resolution transcoding pipeline; edge-CDN-dominant serving; tiered cache with RAM for the very hot 1% and flash for the warm 20%. Pareto was the single most useful constant — without it, "we need to cache everything" gives a wrong-by-100× answer.

06Where the math lies to you

Every common BoE mistake comes from one of five places. Watch for them.

Confusing peak with average

Average QPS is a billing number; peak QPS is a capacity number. Capacity-plan on peak — sometimes 5–10× the average — or your launch will get embarrassed by the first lunchtime spike.

Forgetting replication

"Storage per year" is raw bytes; what you actually provision is raw × replication factor (usually 3) × overhead (indexes, WAL, free space — another 1.5–2×). The disk you order is 4–6× the napkin number.

Using KB/MB for network

Storage is bytes; bandwidth is usually bits. A "1 Gbps" link is 125 MB/s, not 1 GB/s — divide by 8. Mixing them up is an 8× error and silently undersized links.

Ignoring the read/write ratio

Most systems are 10–1000× more reads than writes. Sizing to write QPS without scaling reads accordingly produces a system that runs fine for the first user and falls over at the second.

Missing the hidden multiplier

One user "posting a photo" can fan out to: write the photo, write a row, update an inverted index, update each follower's feed cache, write an audit log, update analytics. One front-door event becomes a dozen back-end writes. Estimate the fan-out before you estimate capacity.

Treating the answer as precise

BoE numbers are within ~10×, not within 10%. Don't argue about whether a system needs 4.7 TB or 5.2 TB — argue about whether it's "a few terabytes" or "a few hundred." Wrong-by-2× is fine; wrong-by-100× is the bug to catch.

And one cross-check that costs nothing: Little's Law. If your BoE says peak QPS = 600 K and average per-request time = 50 ms, then average concurrency = 30 K — meaning you need that many in-flight workers, connections, or cores across the fleet. If a downstream sub-system can't accept 30 K concurrent calls, the BoE just told you about a bottleneck before you built it.

07The one-line takeaway

A few constants and four multiplications turn "an app for X" into the regime of system you're actually building. Do it before you design — not during the post-mortem.

The habit worth forming: every time someone proposes a system, before talking about technologies, do the BoE in your head. How many users? How many actions each? What does that come to per second at peak? How much storage does a year of that take? Within thirty seconds you'll know whether the conversation should be about Postgres or Cassandra, single-region or multi-region, sync or async. The numbers won't be precise — but the regime they put you in will be right.
