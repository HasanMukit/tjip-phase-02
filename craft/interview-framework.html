The 6-Step Interview Framework

System design interviews look open-ended, but the candidates who pass treat them as a structured walk through six well-defined steps.

"Design Twitter." "Design a URL shortener." "Design Uber dispatch." The prompt is always one sentence, and the page on the whiteboard is always blank. The trap is to start drawing boxes — load balancer, app servers, MySQL — before anyone has agreed on what's being built, how big it is, or which of a hundred features the interviewer actually cares about. The framework on this page is the antidote: six steps, in order, with a time-box for each.

This isn't an interview-only tool. The same six steps describe how working engineers approach a real design doc — clarify, size, define the contract, sketch the system, choose the data model, then go deep on the parts that will actually break. The interview is just a 45-minute compression of that real process.

01Why a framework at all

The most common interview failure isn't getting a hard question wrong. It's spending five minutes drawing six boxes — client, load balancer, web servers, cache, database, queue — and then defending those boxes for the next forty. The candidate never asked what the system does. The interviewer never heard them justify a single decision. Forty minutes of motion, zero progress.

A framework prevents that. It forces you to answer cheap questions first (what is this? how big?) before expensive ones (which database? what's the schema?). Each step's output is the next step's input. The boxes-and-arrows diagram you eventually draw is justified because it sits on top of explicit numbers, an explicit API, and explicit non-functional requirements.

The misconception this corrects
Beginners think a system design interview is a test of how many components you know — Kafka, Cassandra, Redis, ELB, S3. It isn't. It's a test of how you reason under ambiguity. The framework shows the interviewer that you don't start with components; you start with the problem, and the components fall out of constraints you established yourself.

02The six steps at a glance

Every step has one job, one artifact, and a time-box. The artifact is what should be visible on the whiteboard when that step ends. If it isn't, you're not done with the step.

Figure 01 — A 45-minute interview, divided

The framework as a stacked timeline. Step 1 (blue) scopes the problem; steps 2–5 (green) design the system — entities, contract, flow, architecture; step 6 (amber) is where the interviewer learns whether you actually understand what you drew. Bar widths reflect time-boxes — note that the deep dive takes a third of the whole interview by itself.

03Step 1 — Requirements

The interviewer's prompt is deliberately under-specified. "Design Twitter" could mean three different systems depending on whether you focus on the timeline read path, the post write path, or the celebrity fan-out problem. Step 1 is where you turn one sentence into a scoped problem statement that you and the interviewer agree on.

You're collecting two kinds of requirements. Functional requirements are what the system does — post a tweet, follow a user, read a timeline. Non-functional requirements are how it has to behave — latency targets, availability, scale, consistency expectations. Both matter; non-functional is what most candidates skip.

Functional questions

Which features are in scope? What does a user actually do? What can we cut? "Are DMs in scope, or just the public timeline?" "Does follow have to be bidirectional?"

Non-functional questions

How many users? Read-heavy or write-heavy? What's the latency budget? How available — three nines or four? Which inconsistencies are tolerable? "Is a 30-second stale timeline OK?"

The non-functional column carries one extra responsibility: rough scale numbers (DAU, peak QPS, payload size, storage/year) belong here. They don't need to be precise — 10× accuracy is enough to fix the regime — but they have to exist before you sketch anything, because the answer to "single MySQL or sharded NoSQL?" is decided by an order of magnitude on these numbers, not by aesthetics. The full estimation method, including the powers-of-two cheat sheet and worked examples, lives on the dedicated back-of-the-envelope page.

Write the answers down on the whiteboard as a numbered list. Two columns — functional on the left, non-functional on the right. This list is the contract you and the interviewer just agreed on. Everything you draw next has to be justifiable against it.

04Step 2 — Core entities

Before you can describe what the API does, you have to name the things it does it to. Step 2 lists the entities — the nouns the system manipulates. For Twitter: User, Tweet, Follow. For Uber dispatch: Rider, Driver, Ride, Location. For an e-commerce checkout: Listing, Cart, Order, Payment, Shipment. Five entities is typical; ten is starting to feel busy.

This isn't the full data model yet — you're not committing to columns, indexes, or primary keys. You're just identifying which objects exist as first-class things in the system. Two reasons that matters:

  • Entities constrain the API. Every endpoint in the next step is a verb on one of these nouns. POST /tweets, GET /users/:id/timeline, DELETE /follows/:id. If an endpoint operates on something that isn't on your entity list, either the entity list is incomplete or the endpoint shouldn't exist.
  • Entities surface the relationships. User — owns — Tweet. User — follows — User. Rider — books — Ride. Drawing the noun graph (a quick boxes-and-lines sketch is enough) reveals which lookups have to be cheap and which can be expensive — information that drives the high-level design two steps later.
What lands on the whiteboard
A short list — five to ten boxes — and a few arrows between them showing the dominant relationships. Don't fill in fields; that's a deep-dive concern. The artifact you want is "here are the things; here's how they connect", nothing more.

05Step 3 — API design

This is where you commit to the system's contract — the public surface that clients call and that everything inside has to support. Endpoints, methods, request and response shapes, error codes, idempotency keys, pagination tokens. Five focused minutes; this isn't a full OpenAPI spec, it's the dozen calls that cover the functional requirements from Step 1.

The discipline here matters because the API forces you to be specific. "Post a tweet" becomes POST /tweets with a body of {text, media_ids} and a response of {tweet_id, created_at}. Now you've committed to a tweet ID type (string? int? UUID? snowflake?), a maximum payload size, a synchronous vs async write, and an error contract. Each of those choices has downstream consequences for the next three steps.

Why API design comes before data flow and high-level design
Because the API is the boundary between client behavior and server architecture. Once you know the calls, you know what state has to be readable, what writes have to be durable, what can be eventually consistent, and what has to fan out. Tracing a request flow without first knowing the calls leaves the flow unconstrained; drawing a load balancer before knowing whether the system has 6 endpoints or 600 is premature.

06Step 4 — Data flow

You have entities and an API. Step 4 is the storyboard: for the two or three most important operations, walk through the request end-to-end and write down what happens, in sequence. "User clicks Buy → request hits the gateway → auth check → orders service writes a row → publishes order_created on Kafka → fulfilment worker picks it up → response returns 201 with order ID." Five minutes; one or two flows in plain language.

This step exists between the API and the architecture for a reason. Writing the flow first surfaces which boxes you'll need in step 5 — a queue (because something is async), a cache (because a hot read is on the critical path), a separate write service (because the read and write paths have different requirements). If you skip straight from API to architecture, you tend to draw the components you've heard of rather than the components the flow demands.

A useful discipline: for each flow, mark what's synchronous (the user is waiting) and what's asynchronous (fire-and-forget, retried in the background). The sync/async split is one of the highest-leverage decisions in the design — it sets which components need to be fast and which only need to be eventually correct.

What lands on the whiteboard
Two or three numbered sequences, written linearly. 1. client → gateway. 2. gateway → auth. 3. ... Don't draw the full architecture diagram yet — that's the next step. Keep the flow in text so the eye doesn't get distracted by boxes.

07Step 5 — High-level design

Now — and only now — you start drawing boxes. Client → CDN → load balancer → app servers → cache → database. Maybe a queue for async work, maybe a separate read-path service, maybe a CDN tier for static assets. Ten minutes; this is the "main diagram" the interviewer remembers.

The goal is a flow-justified diagram, not a technology shopping list. Every box on the diagram should trace back to either an entity (Step 2), an endpoint (Step 3), or a step in one of the request flows (Step 4). For each box, say what it does, not just what it is. "An app server" is weak. "A stateless app tier behind an L7 load balancer that fans the request out to a write path (synchronous, persisted to the primary) and a fan-out worker (async, pushed to followers' inbox cache)" tells the interviewer you've thought about it.

This is also where the storage shape gets committed. The hard questions aren't field names — they're:

SQL or NoSQL?

If your access pattern is "look up by primary key" and your scale is huge, NoSQL. If you need joins, transactions, and ad-hoc queries, SQL. Hybrid is common: SQL for the truth, key-value for hot reads.

What's the partition key?

Once data is sharded, every query either includes the partition key (cheap) or fans out to all shards (expensive). The shard key is often the single highest-leverage decision in the design. Get it wrong and every read is a scatter-gather.

Common trap
Drawing a normalised, third-normal-form ER diagram for a system that you just sized at 30k QPS. At that scale, denormalisation isn't a code smell — it's the design. Duplicate data, store the user's display name on the tweet row, accept that a profile rename is now a fan-out job. The schema follows the access pattern, not vice versa.

This is also where Distribution and Performance show up. Decisions like "do we shard?" lean on consistent hashing; decisions like "what happens during a regional outage?" lean on CAP; decisions about cache, queue, and back-pressure lean on resilience patterns. The framework doesn't replace those topics — it tells you when to apply them.

08Step 6 — Deep dive & bottlenecks

This is the third of the interview that decides the outcome. The interviewer picks one or two components from your high-level diagram and asks you to go deep. "Walk me through what happens when a celebrity with 50M followers posts. Where's the bottleneck? What breaks first?"

You're not done until you've talked about three things for at least one hot component: the bottleneck, the failure mode, and the trade-off. The bottleneck is where load piles up first. The failure mode is what happens when that component is gone (network partition? hot shard? slow disk?). The trade-off is what you'd give up to relieve the pressure.

Figure 02 — Three lenses for the deep dive

For any hot component the interviewer probes, name the bottleneck (where load piles up), the failure mode (what happens when it's gone), and the trade-off (what you'd accept to relieve it). Hitting all three on a single component is what "deep dive" actually means.

Specific tools to reach for during the deep dive:

  • The bottleneck rule — total throughput is set by the slowest stage. See throughput.
  • Little's Law — concurrency = throughput × latency. Use it to size pools, queues, and worker counts. See latency & throughput.
  • p99 not average — the tail is what users feel. Hedged requests, redundant fan-out, request coalescing. See latency.
  • Resilience patterns — retries with jitter, circuit breakers, bulkheads, load shedding. See resilience patterns.
  • CAP for partitions — pick CP or AP per operation, not per system. See CAP.

The strongest signal you can give in this step isn't naming a famous tool — it's saying "the bottleneck here is X. If we relieve it with Y, we pay Z. I'd rather pay Z than X because this part of Step 1's requirements." Closing the loop back to the requirements is what separates senior reasoning from junior pattern-matching.

09Time-boxing & pacing

The time-boxes in Figure 01 aren't suggestions. They're the difference between finishing the interview and getting cut off mid-architecture with no deep dive done. Two pacing rules to internalise:

Steps 1–4 are cheap. Don't overspend. You can blow ten minutes on requirements and feel productive — you're talking, the interviewer is nodding — but you've used 22% of your time on a step that should have taken 11%. If a requirement is genuinely ambiguous, write down two interpretations and move on. You can come back.

Step 6 is where the signal is. Protect it. Most candidates run out of time in Step 5. The fix isn't to talk faster in Step 5; it's to spend less in Steps 1–4. The interviewer is going to grade you mostly on what you said in the deep dive. A polished requirements list with no deep dive is a fail. A scrappy architecture diagram with a strong deep dive is a pass.

Pacing for longer or shorter slots
60-min interview: scale up roughly 7 / 7 / 7 / 7 / 12 / 20. The deep dive grows the most.
30-min screen: skim Step 4 (mention the data flow inline as you draw Step 5) and aim for 4 / 4 / 4 / — / 8 / 10.
In all cases, Step 6 is the largest single block. If you find yourself shrinking it, you're shrinking the wrong step.

10The one-line takeaway

Scope before you sketch, sketch before you go deep — and protect the deep dive at all costs.

That's the framework in one sentence. Step 1 scopes the problem. Steps 2–5 sketch the system — entities, contract, flow, architecture. Step 6 is where the interviewer learns whether you understood what you drew. Every minute you save in the early steps is a minute you can spend in the last one, and the last one is what gets you hired.

The framework looks rigid on paper, but it isn't. It's a default — a path of least resistance through a problem you've never seen before. Once you've used it in five or six designs, you'll start jumping between steps fluidly: a Step-6 failure mode forces you to revisit a Step-4 box, a Step-2 number invalidates a Step-1 requirement. That's normal. The framework isn't a script. It's a checklist that makes sure none of the load-bearing questions go unanswered.
