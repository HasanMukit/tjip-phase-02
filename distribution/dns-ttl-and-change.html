DNS TTL & cutovers

How to change a DNS record without leaving users stranded for hours — the four-phase playbook, the dig commands that prove it worked, and the gotchas that bite you anyway.

DNS is a giant distributed cache. The hardest problem in any cache is invalidation — telling every cached copy that the answer just changed. DNS doesn't try to push invalidations across the internet. Instead, every record carries a TTL — a deadline after which caches must re-fetch — and the whole system relies on those deadlines expiring to move stale answers out of circulation.

That single design choice is the source of every "I changed my DNS but half my users still see the old IP" story. Understanding how to change a record well is, at heart, understanding how to manipulate that cache window before, during, and after the change.

01TTL is a lease — and who sets it

Every DNS record carries a TTL in seconds. When a resolver caches the record, that TTL is how long it's allowed to keep using the cached answer before re-asking the authoritative server. After expiry, the next query re-fetches. Until expiry, the cached answer is what gets served — even if the authoritative answer has already changed.

That last sentence is the whole story of DNS propagation delay.

Who sets the TTL

The zone administrator — i.e., whoever owns the domain — sets the TTL on each record at the authoritative server. It's not a global system parameter; it's a per-record value baked into the record itself. When you edit a record at Cloudflare / Route 53 / Namecheap, the TTL field next to "value" is what you're setting.

Two extra knobs live at the zone level rather than the record level:

  • The zone's SOA record carries a default TTL that records inherit if not specified explicitly.
  • The SOA also carries a negative-cache TTL (the MINIMUM field) that controls how long NXDOMAIN answers are cached. This is the clock that bites you if a record briefly disappears during a botched cutover.

Resolvers and clients are expected to honour the configured TTL — but with caveats. Some recursive resolvers floor very low values (refusing TTLs below 30 or 60 s); some clients (Chrome, certain JVM versions) cap very high values; and the browser, OS, and router caches all sit between the authority and the user, each with its own enforcement. The configured TTL is your intent; what actually happens in any given cache is a negotiation between that intent and whatever implementation is reading it.

02Why naive changes break

You're moving api.example.com from one IP to another. You edit the record at your DNS provider and wait. What happens?

  • The authoritative server now serves the new IP.
  • Every recursive resolver that already cached the old IP keeps serving the old IP for up to its full TTL.
  • If the steady-state TTL was 86400 (24 h), some users won't see the new IP for a full day.

That's the bug. The fix is to make the cache window small before you change anything, do the change inside that small window, then make the window large again once it's stable. Four phases.

03Two TTLs when a CNAME is involved

The bug above was about one record's TTL. The picture gets more interesting when the name resolves through a CNAME chain, because every record on the chain has its own TTL — and those clocks tick independently.

Concrete example. Your blog is hosted on a managed service:

blog.example.com. 3600 IN CNAME target.cloud.com. target.cloud.com. 60 IN A 192.0.2.42

A resolver doing a fresh lookup gets back both records and caches each independently. Their TTLs apply separately:

Figure 01 — Two clocks on a CNAME chain

The CNAME at blog.example.com and the A record at target.cloud.com are cached separately. The A's clock ticks fast; the CNAME's clock ticks slowly. Each refresh only re-fetches the link that expired.

Independent expiry, independent refresh

After 60 s the A record at target.cloud.com is stale and the resolver re-fetches just the A — it still knows from cache that blog.example.com is an alias of target.cloud.com. Only when the CNAME's 3600 s elapses does the resolver re-walk the whole chain.

What this means in practice

  • Changing the IP that the target points to propagates within the target's TTL (60 s). The CNAME doesn't have to expire — caches will pick up the new A on its next refresh.
  • Changing where the CNAME points (different target altogether) propagates within the CNAME's TTL (3600 s). The new target's A record is irrelevant to a cache that hasn't refreshed the CNAME yet.
  • For any change that requires re-walking the full chain, the slowest TTL bounds your cutover.

Why managed services love this shape

This split-clock behaviour is the architectural reason CNAMEs to managed services work so well:

  • Heroku, AWS ELB, Cloudflare, Vercel set very low TTLs (~60 s) on their A records, so they can rotate IPs at will without coordinating with customers.
  • You set a longer TTL (1 h or more) on your CNAME, because you rarely change which provider you point at — only which IPs that provider rotates through.
  • End-user resolution is cheap (one CNAME lookup per hour) but the provider can still move IPs in a minute. Both sides win without negotiating.

Each link in the chain has its own clock — only the link you change starts ticking.

04The four-phase playbook

Figure 02 — TTL across the four phases

The procedure spans many hours, but the user-visible disruption window — the time between the change and global propagation — is bounded by the new low TTL, not the old one.

Phase 1 — Pre-lower the TTL, then wait one old TTL

Drop the record's TTL to a small value (60 s is the convention; 30 s is fine; 300 s is the cautious upper bound). Then wait at least one full old TTL.

The waiting step is the one that gets skipped, and it's the one that matters. Here's why: a resolver that cached the record yesterday saw the old TTL of 86400, and it will hold that record for up to 24 h regardless of what you change at the authority today. Lowering the TTL only affects caches that re-fetch after the lowering. So you have to wait long enough for every existing cached copy to expire and be re-fetched with the new low TTL.

After this phase, every resolver in the world is holding a copy that says "good for 60 s." Your cache window has shrunk from a day to a minute.

Phase 2 — Change the record, verify against authority

Update the record at the authoritative server. Immediately confirm the change landed by querying the authoritative server directly — this bypasses every cache:

dig api.example.com @ns1.example.com +short # → 198.51.100.42 (the new IP)

If the authority reports the new IP, the change is real. Public resolvers will still show the old IP for up to the new low TTL (~60 s) — that's expected, not a bug.

Phase 3 — Wait one new TTL, then verify propagation

Wait at least the new low TTL period. Every cache that holds a stale copy will refresh on its next request and pick up the new IP. Spot-check with multiple public resolvers to confirm global propagation:

for ns in 8.8.8.8 1.1.1.1 9.9.9.9 208.67.222.222; do echo -n "$ns → "; dig api.example.com @$ns +short done

If they all return the new IP, propagation is done.

Phase 4 — Raise the TTL back up

Once the change is stable (give it longer than the bare minimum — an hour is a comfortable cushion), raise the TTL back to its steady-state value (typical: 1 h to 24 h). Why bother:

  • Low TTL means every cache re-fetches every minute, so your authoritative servers carry roughly TTL_old / TTL_new times the query load they had before. For a 1-day → 60 s drop that's a 1440× multiplier.
  • First-hit users at each cache pay the full recursion cost (root → TLD → authoritative) on every expiry — bad p99 for cold paths.
  • Raising back is hygiene: low TTL is for cutovers, not for steady state.

A concrete timeline

Time Action What caches hold
T−25 h Steady state. TTL = 86400. old IP, ≤ 24 h
T−24 h Lower TTL to 60 (nothing else changes yet). old IP, ≤ 24 h
T−0 24 h has passed. Every cache holds TTL = 60. old IP, ≤ 60 s
T−0 Change the IP. Verify with dig @ns1. old IP, ≤ 60 s
T+60 s Caches refresh. Spot-check public resolvers. new IP, ≤ 60 s
T+1 h Stable. Raise TTL back to 86400. new IP, ≤ 24 h

The whole thing takes ~25 hours wall-clock for a 1-day TTL, but the user-visible disruption window is bounded by the new low TTL — about a minute.

05Verifying with dig

After changing a record you want to answer two different questions, and they need different tools:

  1. Did the authoritative server accept my change? Query the authoritative server directly, bypassing every cache.
  2. Has the change propagated to the rest of the world yet? Query a public resolver and watch its TTL count down.

dig is the standard tool. The invocations worth memorising:

# Default — uses the system resolver, just like a browser would dig www.example.com # Just the answer, nothing else dig www.example.com +short # Ask a specific resolver (Google's public DNS in this case) dig www.example.com @8.8.8.8 # Ask the authoritative server DIRECTLY — bypasses every cache. # This is the move for "did my change actually land?" dig www.example.com @ns1.example.com # Show the full recursion: root → TLD → authoritative dig www.example.com +trace # Specific record types dig example.com NS # who's authoritative for this zone dig example.com MX # mail servers dig example.com TXT # SPF / DKIM / verification dig example.com SOA # zone metadata (TTL defaults, serial number)

The "is my change live?" workflow

# 1. Find the zone's authoritative nameservers. dig example.com NS +short # → ns1.example.com. # ns2.example.com. # 2. Ask one of them directly. If this returns the new value, your DNS provider # has accepted the change. (No cache between you and the source of truth.) dig www.example.com @ns1.example.com +short # 3. Now ask a public resolver. If this still shows the old value, the change # is real but caches are still serving stale data. Watch the TTL count down # on repeated queries — when it hits 0 the next query will refetch. dig www.example.com @8.8.8.8

The only way to know the truth is to ask the authoritative server.

Every other answer is some cache's opinion, and might be minutes or hours behind. If you skip step 2 and panic-debug from step 3 only, you'll waste an afternoon thinking your change didn't go through when really it just hadn't propagated yet.

Reading dig output

;; ANSWER SECTION: www.example.com. 287 IN A 93.184.216.34 ^^^ TTL remaining in seconds

The TTL shown is what the resolver you queried has left on its cached copy — it counts down on repeated queries. Hit 0 and the next query refetches from upstream. Querying the authoritative server shows the full configured TTL (because there's no cache between you and the source).

Alternatives: nslookup (older, more terse output) and host (simpler, less info). dig is the one to learn — it's what every DNS doc and incident-runbook will assume.

06The low-vs-high TTL trade-off

Low TTL (60 s)

Fast propagation — good for cutovers and failover. But every cache re-fetches frequently, so authoritative-server load goes up and first-hit users at every cache pay the full recursion cost.

High TTL (24 h)

Slow propagation — cutovers take forever. But most queries are served from cache, authoritative load is minimal, and end-user resolution is sub-millisecond.

The common shape: keep TTL high during steady state (1–24 h), drop it to 30–60 s before a planned change, raise it again after. Steady state optimises for serving; pre-lower optimises for changing.

07Gotchas the playbook doesn't fix

  • Long-lived connections pin to the old IP. DNS resolution happens at connect time only. WebSockets, gRPC streams, and HTTP/2 keepalive connections that opened before the change keep talking to the old IP until they reconnect. Run both IPs in parallel during the cutover — don't tear down the old one the moment the DNS change goes in.
  • Some clients ignore TTL. Chrome caps DNS TTL at ~60 s regardless of what you set; some Java clients cache DNS for the JVM lifetime by default (networkaddress.cache.ttl); some load balancers and SDKs do their own DNS pinning. Low TTL is a hint, not a guarantee.
  • Some recursive resolvers floor the TTL. A resolver may refuse to respect TTLs below 30 s or 60 s. You can't make global propagation faster than the slowest resolver in the chain.
  • Negative caching is its own clock. If the record briefly disappears (NXDOMAIN), that absence is also cached, governed by the SOA's MINIMUM field, not your record's TTL. A botched intermediate state can poison caches for hours. Never delete-then-recreate; always replace in place.
  • OS-level caches need manual flushing during debugging. sudo dscacheutil -flushcache; sudo killall -HUP mDNSResponder on macOS, sudo systemd-resolve --flush-caches on Linux, ipconfig /flushdns on Windows. Useful for you, not for users.

08The one-line takeaway

A DNS change is bounded by whatever TTL was in effect when caches picked up the previous answer — pre-lowering is how you shrink that bound before you need it.

Everything else — the wait, the dig commands, the four phases — is just the discipline of moving the cache window before, during, and after the cutover. Skip the pre-lower and the change still happens, but users notice it for as long as the steady-state TTL allows. Get the discipline right and a global IP migration becomes a one-minute event nobody sees.
