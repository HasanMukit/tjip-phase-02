Performance under pressure

Eight production-shaped scenarios that exercise the latency hierarchy, throughput bottlenecks, Little's Law, and the tail. Read the problem, understand the terms, think about what you'd do — then reveal the solution.

Latency and throughput numbers don't lie — but they do mislead when you read them without the model behind them. Each case study below gives you the scenario, the diagnosis, and the vocabulary to reason about it. The fix is hidden behind a reveal; try to form your own answer before you open it.

01The dashboard is green but users say it's slow

Scenario
Your product page calls 8 internal services in parallel and joins the results. Dashboards show p50 = 40 ms per backend call. Support tickets say pages feel "laggy" and sometimes time out. Engineering says the graphs look perfect.

What's happening. The engineers and the users are looking at different numbers. The dashboard shows the median latency of one call at a time. The user experiences the maximum of 8 concurrent calls — the page can't render until the slowest one returns. Even if each call's p99 is "only" 800 ms, the probability that at least one of the 8 hits the tail is 1 − 0.99⁸ ≈ 7.7%. So the page's effective p90 is whatever the backend's p99 is. This is the tail-at-scale effect: when many calls fan out, the worst call dominates what the user sees.

Terms to know
Latency
The end-to-end time for one request, from send to response received. Measured per request.
Percentile (p50, p95, p99, p99.9)
"What's the worst latency the fastest X% of requests saw?" p99 = 500 ms means 99% of requests finished in 500 ms or less; the other 1% were slower.
Median vs average
The median (p50) is the midpoint; the average can be pulled upward by a long tail. A latency distribution with a long tail has a much higher average than median.
Fan-out
A single user request triggering N parallel downstream calls. The user waits for the slowest of the N to return.
Tail latency / long tail
The slow end of the latency distribution — the small fraction of requests dramatically slower than typical, caused by GC pauses, retries, cache misses, slow network paths, etc.
Tail-at-scale
Coined by Jeff Dean. In an N-way fan-out, the probability that at least one call hits the tail grows quickly with N, so the user-facing latency is dominated by the slowest tail of N calls, not by the median of any single call.
Reveal the solution Two changes: measure the right number, and cut the tail at request time.

Solution. Two changes, in order:

  1. Stop trusting p50. Alert on p99 for every fan-out service. A regression in the tail is invisible to the median until it's catastrophic.
  2. Cut the tail at request time. Use hedged requests: once a call has been out for p95 of its normal latency without answering, send a second copy to a different replica and take whichever returns first. Google's "Tail at Scale" paper shows this drops aggregate p99 by 30–40% for a few percent extra load.

For an N-way fan-out, the user's latency is the maximum of N tails, not the average.

02A payment API over its 100 ms budget

Scenario
Product says authorize-card must return in ≤ 100 ms end-to-end. Current p95 is 240 ms. A flamegraph shows 180 ms spent in one call: a synchronous read of the cardholder's risk profile from a database in a different region.

What's happening. You've blown your latency budget inside a single network hop. Light takes roughly 5 ms per 1,000 km; a cross-continent round-trip is bounded below by physics at ~60–150 ms. Your 180 ms is already close to the minimum-possible cost of a cross-region call — there's no amount of code optimization that gets you from "cross-continent hop" to "100 ms budget," because the hop alone is most of the budget. The fix has to avoid the hop, not speed it up.

Terms to know
Latency budget
An upper bound on how long a request is allowed to take end-to-end, usually set by product or SLO requirements. Work backwards from the budget to allocate per-stage targets.
Flamegraph
A visualization of where time goes inside a request — each horizontal bar is a function, its width proportional to how long that function took. Reveals which call dominates the latency.
Round-trip time (RTT)
How long a packet takes to go to a remote host and a response to come back. Same-DC RTT: ~500 µs. Cross-region RTT: 60–150 ms. Set by physics and fiber topology, not by your code.
The 1000× hierarchy
Each storage/network tier is roughly 1000× slower than the one above. L1 cache (~1 ns) → RAM (~80 ns) → NVMe (~30 µs) → same-DC network (~500 µs) → cross-region (~100 ms). Crossing a tier is orders of magnitude, not percentage points.
Read replica
A read-only copy of a database in another region, kept up to date asynchronously. Reads from it are local (fast); writes still go to the primary.
Write-through cache
A local cache that sits in front of a slower authoritative store and absorbs reads. On a miss, it fetches and fills; on a write, it updates both the cache and the store.
Reveal the solution Two ways to avoid the cross-region hop, from cheapest to most effort.

Solution. Replace the cross-region read with an in-region cache or replica. Two good options:

Read-only replica in-region

Async-replicate the risk profile table to a read-replica in the authorize-card region. Read latency drops from ~180 ms to ~1–2 ms. Stale reads by a few hundred ms are acceptable here — risk scores don't swing that fast.

Local write-through cache

Keep the primary where it is; cache each cardholder's profile locally with a short TTL and refresh-ahead on access. Hit rate > 95% means nearly all reads are microseconds.

The general lesson: no amount of code optimization can cross a tier boundary. If your latency budget forbids a cross-region hop, don't do one — move or replicate the data.

03A Kafka consumer that can't keep up

Scenario
A topic receives 40,000 events/sec. Your consumer group processes them by inserting one row per event into Postgres. You've added partitions (24 → 48) and consumers (12 → 24), but throughput plateaus at ~18,000 events/sec and lag grows by 22,000/sec every second.

What's happening. Adding consumers didn't help, which is the first diagnostic clue: the bottleneck isn't on the consumer side. Profile the database — it's doing 18,000 tiny commits/sec and its write-ahead log (WAL) flush is saturated. That's the slowest stage of the pipeline. The bottleneck rule says a pipeline's throughput equals the throughput of its slowest stage, no matter how fast everything else is. Adding parsers, consumers, or partitions upstream of a saturated writer produces zero throughput gain; it just grows the backlog faster.

Terms to know
Throughput
Units of work completed per unit time (events/sec, requests/sec, MB/s). A system-wide number, distinct from per-request latency.
Kafka topic / partition
A topic is a named stream of events. It's split into partitions; each partition is an ordered log. Partitions are the unit of parallelism — one consumer reads from each assigned partition.
Consumer group
A set of consumers cooperating to consume a topic. Kafka assigns each partition to exactly one member of the group, so adding consumers scales throughput only up to the partition count.
Consumer lag
How many events the consumer is behind the producer. Lag that grows linearly in time means offered rate > consumer rate — you're falling behind forever.
Bottleneck
The slowest stage in a pipeline. System throughput is whatever this stage can do; investment anywhere else is zero-impact until it's no longer the slowest.
Write-ahead log (WAL)
A durability mechanism in databases: every change is first appended to a log on disk before being applied. Commit latency is bounded by how fast the log can fsync.
Batching
Amortizing per-call overhead across many units of work. 1,000 rows in one COPY costs only slightly more than one row in one INSERT — throughput per second rises dramatically.
Little's Law
L = λ × W. For any stable system: average in-flight work (L) = arrival rate (λ) × time inside (W). Relates throughput, latency, and concurrency unconditionally.
Reveal the solution Move the DB into a regime it's good at — bulk writes.

Solution. Move the DB into a regime it's good at — bulk writes — by batching on the consumer:

  • Buffer events for up to 50 ms or 1,000 rows, whichever comes first.
  • Flush with a single COPY or a multi-row INSERT ... VALUES (...), (...), ....
  • Postgres goes from ~18k single-row inserts/sec to >100k batched rows/sec on the same hardware.

This is a classic latency-for-throughput trade. Each event now waits up to 50 ms longer, but the system can keep up with offered load instead of falling behind forever.

Sanity check with Little's Law
To absorb 40,000 events/sec end-to-end, if each batched flush takes 50 ms, you need L = λ × W = 40,000 × 0.05 = 2,000 events in-flight. With batches of 1,000 rows, that's 2 concurrent flushes per partition — well within any worker pool. The numbers add up; the original design's didn't.

04The autoscaler that thrashes at the knee

Scenario
Your HTTP service is capacity-planned at 1,000 RPS per box. Autoscaler is wired to "scale out when CPU > 80%." Every traffic spike still produces a 60-second window of timeouts and 5xx errors before new instances warm up.

What's happening. CPU is a lagging indicator near the knee of the latency–throughput curve. Before CPU saturates, requests have already started queuing — the machine is fully occupied serving the front of the queue while p99 climbs sharply behind it. CPU reads as "fine" because the CPU is genuinely busy doing real work; it just isn't getting to newer requests in time. By the time CPU hits 80%, the queue is already tens of seconds deep and scaling out is too late. You're reacting to a symptom that appears after the damage is done.

Terms to know
Requests per second (RPS)
Throughput unit for HTTP services. Count completed requests in a window, divide.
The knee of the curve
The point on the load-vs-latency curve where adding just a little more load causes latency to shoot up sharply. Left of the knee: linear. Right of the knee: exponential and recoverable only by shedding load.
Queue depth
The number of requests waiting to be served. Queues grow when arrival rate exceeds service rate; queue time adds directly to latency for every request stuck behind others.
Lagging vs leading indicator
Lagging: fires after the problem is visible (CPU saturation). Leading: fires before (p95 rising, queue growing). Autoscaling on leading indicators leaves time for instances to warm up.
Load shedding
Deliberately refusing excess requests (HTTP 429) rather than queuing them. A refused request is cheap; a queued request that eventually times out wastes a worker slot.
Concurrency limit / bulkhead
A hard cap on how many requests can be in-flight at once per process or per host. Protects the system from accepting more work than it can finish at target latency.
Reveal the solution Scale on a leading signal, shed at the door.

Solution. Two fixes layered together:

Scale on a leading signal

Trigger scale-out on p95 latency or queue depth, not CPU. Both rise left of the knee, before throughput collapses. Target: scale when p95 hits 1.5× baseline, not when CPU hits 80%.

Shed load at the door

Put a concurrency limit (e.g. 1.2× per-box capacity) in front of the service. Beyond that, return 429 Too Many Requests immediately. A refused request is cheap; a queued-then-timed-out request wastes a worker slot twice.

The deeper point: autoscaling reacts to a symptom. Load shedding prevents the symptom. You want both — the shedder keeps you out of the overloaded zone while the scaler catches up.

05A 7-hour ETL we need to finish in 90 minutes

Scenario
Nightly ETL ingests 200M rows from an upstream API into a warehouse. It currently takes 7 hours. Leadership wants it under 90 minutes so morning dashboards don't lag. Your intern says "make each insert faster."

What's happening. The intern is optimizing the wrong number. At 200M rows / 7 h that's ~8,000 rows/sec. Each row-at-a-time INSERT pays a fixed overhead for round-trip, transaction setup, index maintenance, and WAL flush — that overhead dominates the per-row cost, and shaving the tiny compute portion produces tiny gains. ETL is a throughput problem: what matters is total wall-clock time, not per-row latency. The right lever is to do more work per round-trip, not to make each round-trip faster.

Terms to know
ETL
Extract, Transform, Load. A batch pipeline that moves data from one system (transactional DB, API, file drop) into another (warehouse, analytics store).
COPY vs INSERT
Postgres COPY streams many rows over a single connection with minimal per-row overhead. INSERT processes one row (or a small VALUES list) per statement, with full per-statement overhead.
Batching (revisit)
Amortizing fixed per-call overhead across many rows. If per-INSERT overhead is 1 ms and each row's real work is 10 µs, batching 1,000 rows per call gives 100× throughput.
Parallelism vs concurrency
Parallelism: actually running N things on N cores/threads at once. Concurrency: having N things in flight at once, possibly interleaved. For ETL, partition the input so N workers can run in true parallel.
Index maintenance
Every INSERT also updates every index on the table. B-tree updates cost log(N) per row — noticeable at scale. Dropping and rebuilding indexes around a bulk load is often a 2–5× win.
Latency-for-throughput trade
Batching, buffering, and pipelining raise aggregate throughput by making individual items wait longer. Appropriate when wall-clock total matters; wrong when individual responsiveness matters.
Reveal the solution Three levers, in order of impact.

Solution. ETL is a throughput problem, not a latency problem. Three levers, in order of impact:

  1. Batch writes. Swap row-at-a-time INSERTs for COPY in chunks of 10k–100k rows. One round-trip amortized over tens of thousands of rows. Easily 50–100× throughput.
  2. Parallelize by shard. Split the 200M rows into 16 independent ranges, 16 workers do COPY concurrently to a partitioned target.
  3. Defer indexes and constraints. Drop secondary indexes during ingest, rebuild at the end. B-tree maintenance per row is a hidden slow stage.

Each individual row now takes longer (it waits for its batch partner). Wall-clock drops from 7 h to well under 90 min. This is the batching trade-off writ large: ETL doesn't care about per-row latency, only total wall-clock.

06The cold-cache spike on every deploy

Scenario
Every time you deploy, p99 latency spikes 10× for about 60 seconds, then settles back to normal. Users complain. Ops says "the cache is cold after deploy" and shrugs. It's been 18 months.

What's happening. A warm in-process cache serves from RAM (~80 ns). A cold cache falls through to the database (~5 ms) or even cross-region (~100 ms). That's a 60,000× to 1,000,000× latency jump per cache-missing request. When you deploy, every new instance starts with an empty cache, so every request for the first 60 seconds pays the next tier's cost. The users hitting the new instances see the full tier jump; the users on warm instances see nothing. The p99 graph spikes until every instance has filled its cache from live traffic.

Terms to know
In-process cache
A cache that lives in the application process's own memory. Zero network cost per hit; lost on restart/deploy.
Shared cache
A cache that lives in a separate process (Redis, Memcached), shared across all app instances. One network hop per hit; survives app restarts.
Cache warm-up
The period after startup during which a cold cache is being filled. Hit rate starts at zero, climbs asymptotically toward steady-state.
Tier boundary
A jump between storage tiers in the 1000× hierarchy (RAM → SSD → network → cross-region). Crossing one is orders of magnitude, not percent.
Canary / gradual rollout
Deploy to a small fraction of instances first, watch metrics, then expand. Limits blast radius and keeps most of the fleet warm during rollout.
Stale-while-revalidate
A caching pattern: serve the last known value immediately even if it's expired, and refresh it in the background. Users never wait on a miss.
Reveal the solution Three options, pick based on engineering budget.

Solution. Three options — pick based on how much work you're willing to do:

Pre-warm, then switch

Start the new instance, replay last-N-minutes of traffic from logs or tee live traffic to it for 30 s, then swap it into the live pool only once its hit rate is above a threshold. No user ever hits a cold instance.

Shared cache (Redis / Memcached)

Move hot state out of in-process caches and into a shared tier. Deploys now rotate workers, not caches. Per-hit latency rises slightly (one network hop) but the cold-start problem disappears.

If neither is feasible, the minimum mitigation is gradual rollout — replace 10% of instances at a time instead of all at once, so warm instances carry the load while new ones fill their caches. It's the "stale-while-revalidate" idea, generalized to process startup.

07"12k RPS" that is actually 8k

Scenario
Your product team celebrates "we now handle 12,000 RPS, up from 9,000." A week later, downstream services complain they see bursts far above 12k. Poking at the traces, you notice ~30% of requests are retries of requests that timed out upstream.

What's happening. Raw throughput counts every response — successes, errors, and retries alike. When the system is near saturation, timeouts trigger client retries. Those retries add load, which makes things slower, which causes more timeouts, which triggers more retries: a positive-feedback loop called retry amplification. Your headline "12k RPS" is really 8,400 real + 3,600 retries, and the retries aren't neutral — they're actively hurting the system by consuming capacity that would have served real work. The number going up is not the progress it looks like.

Terms to know
Goodput
Throughput counting only successful, unique, useful responses — no errors, no retries, no duplicates. The number that actually matches business value.
Retry amplification
A feedback loop where timeouts cause clients to retry, the retries add load, the added load causes more timeouts. Can double or triple offered load during partial failures.
Jittered exponential backoff
A retry schedule with two properties: delays double each retry (exponential), and each delay has random noise added (jitter). Prevents all clients from retrying in synchronized waves.
Circuit breaker
A client-side protection: after N consecutive failures to an endpoint, stop calling it for a cool-down period. Gives the downstream time to recover instead of pounding it.
Idempotency key
A unique token a client sends with each operation; the server deduplicates repeated requests with the same key. Makes retries safe for non-idempotent operations like "charge card."
Partial failure
A state where some requests succeed and others fail. Often worse than a full outage because automation (retries, health checks) reacts to the failures and multiplies them.
Reveal the solution Fix the feedback loop, then measure honestly.

Solution. Fix the feedback loop, then measure honestly:

  1. Jittered exponential backoff on the client side: retries spread in time rather than piling up in the same 100 ms window.
  2. Circuit breakers: after N timeouts, the client stops retrying this endpoint for 30 s. Gives the server a chance to drain.
  3. Idempotency keys so a safely retryable request doesn't double-charge or double-send.
  4. Report goodput on the dashboard alongside throughput. When they diverge, that gap is a red flag, not a rounding error.

A refused request is cheap. A queued-then-timed-out request wastes capacity twice.

See: retries — storm, capping, backoff, jitter.

08The connection pool that looks big but isn't

Scenario
Your app-server connection pool to Postgres is sized at 100. At peak, QPS is 2,000 and average query latency is 50 ms. The service feels fine — until a query plan regression pushes average latency to 80 ms, and suddenly everything slows down, not just the queries that got slower.

What's happening. Apply Little's Law: L = λ × W = 2,000 × 0.05 = 100. Your pool size was exactly the concurrency your workload required — zero headroom. When average latency rose from 50 ms to 80 ms, required concurrency jumped to 2,000 × 0.08 = 160. The pool can only hold 100, so the other 60 queries now queue before the DB. Queuing time adds to every query's wall-clock latency. As wall-clock latency grows, required concurrency grows more, which makes the queue grow. A small increase in W cascaded into a traffic jam affecting every query, not just the slow ones.

Terms to know
Connection pool
A fixed-size set of reusable database connections the app borrows from. Bounded because each connection costs memory and compute on the DB side.
Queries per second (QPS)
Throughput unit for databases. Same idea as RPS but at the DB layer.
Little's Law (revisit)
L = λ × W. Concurrency equals throughput times latency. Given any two, the third is forced; no system can violate this.
Query plan
The strategy the database picks to execute a query — which indexes to use, join order, etc. A plan regression is when the planner picks a worse strategy after a stats update, data growth, or schema change.
Queueing
Waiting for a resource that's fully occupied. Queue time adds directly to latency. Little's Law applies to the queue too — if the queue fills faster than it drains, wait times grow without bound.
PgBouncer / connection multiplexer
A proxy that sits between app and DB, maintaining many cheap "client-side" connections over few expensive "server-side" connections. Decouples app pool size from DB connection limit.
Reveal the solution Three knobs; Little's Law tells you which to move.

Solution. You have three knobs and the relationship L = λ × W constrains them. Move whichever is cheapest:

Lever Move Cost
Lower W Fix the slow queries (index, rewrite, cache) Best — attacks the root cause
Raise L Enlarge pool (150–200) Easy, but only to the DB's own concurrency limit
Lower λ Shed load (429), add pg-bouncer for multiplexing Last resort; protects the pool from overload

The general lesson: any capacity claim involving two of (concurrency, throughput, latency) can be sanity-checked by computing the third. Most "why is it slow?" mysteries dissolve the moment you write L = λ × W on the whiteboard.

See: Little's Law.

09The pattern across all eight

Four habits come out of these eight cases, and they're the whole working playbook:

  1. Name the unit. p50? p99? RPS? Goodput? Most arguments evaporate when everyone is quoting the same number.
  2. Locate the slowest stage. Optimization anywhere else doesn't move the system. Measure utilization per stage; invest in the saturated one.
  3. Check Little's Law. If concurrency, throughput, and latency don't multiply correctly, one of them is wrong — and usually it's the one you didn't measure.
  4. Respect the hierarchy. Crossing a tier boundary (RAM → SSD → network → cross-region) is orders of magnitude, not percentage points. Budget accordingly.

Every performance problem you'll ever chase will turn out to be a variation of one of these eight. The numbers change; the shape doesn't.
